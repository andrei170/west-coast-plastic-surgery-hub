The Three Site Bottlenecks

What blocks the citations, ranked by leverage.

Three structural gaps. Each one is identified, scoped, and fixable in weeks. The credentials are not in question. The infrastructure is.

Highest Leverage to Fix
01

Zero schema markup anywhere on the site

We inspected the homepage, the About page, and the 6 priority procedure pages. None of them carry a single line of JSON-LD structured data. No MedicalBusiness, no Physician, no MedicalProcedure, no FAQPage, no BreadcrumbList, no AggregateRating. This is the direct AEO blocker. Without it, Google's AI cannot reliably cite your credentials, your procedures, your reviews, or your FAQ answers, because nothing on the page tells the AI those facts in a machine-readable format.

Cost of Doing Nothing

Every week the schema does not exist is another week Sessa, Hamwi, and HK keep splitting the seven AI citations between themselves. The cosmetic surgery search volume in Sarasota does not pause. The patients running these searches are converting on the practices Google's AI can cite, because those practices are the only ones it has the structured data to quote.

Fix: MedicalBusiness + Physician + MedicalProcedure + FAQPage + BreadcrumbList + AggregateRating schema deployed across homepage + About + 6 priority procedure pages. 5 to 7 working days. Validates through Google's Rich Results Test before push.
Quickest Conversion Fix
02

No financing partners named above the fold

Your procedures price between $6,000 and $17,000+. The homepage does not name a single financing partner above the fold. The procedure pages publish the price range without a "from as low as $X/month" anchor next to it. The "Financing" link in the nav opens a generic page with no logos, no calculator, no decision-tree.

CareCredit, PatientFi, Cherry, and Alphaeon Credit are names patients already recognise from their dentist, their dermatologist, and their veterinarian. Putting those four logos above the fold converts the same patient at the same price, because the price-shock objection is removed before the patient ever sees the price.

Cost of Doing Nothing

Every consultation form abandoned because the patient scanned to find a price, saw "$8,500 to $12,000," and could not find a financing reassurance in the same scroll is a consultation walked off the page. Cosmetic surgery sites that surface partner logos plus a calculator above the form typically lift consult-form completion by 12 to 28%. The fix is HTML and CSS work, not a rebuild.

Fix: hero trust strip + financing partner row + procedure-page financing block + payment calculator. CareCredit, PatientFi, Cherry, Alphaeon Credit, named explicitly above the fold. TILA-compliant footnote.
Compound Pickup
03

No editorial content, nothing for AI engines to cite

The procedure pages are well-built. Each runs ~2,800 words, has FAQs, has before/after pairs, has a price range. That is the commercial layer. The educational layer, the long-form content that Google's AI pulls from when a patient asks "what is a mommy makeover" or "how do I choose a plastic surgeon in Sarasota" or "what is the recovery for breast augmentation," does not exist on the site at all.

This is the layer Sessa, Hamwi, and HK use to capture pre-consult research traffic. A patient runs the educational query, lands on the competitor's blog post, reads for nine minutes, and has already started a relationship with that practice before they ever ran the commercial-intent search.

Cost of Doing Nothing

Every educational query a Sarasota patient runs while researching cosmetic surgery is currently being answered by a competitor's blog post or by Google's AI quoting a competitor's site. That is the warm-research segment of the funnel walking past you, daily, for as long as the educational content does not exist.

Fix: 4 long-form editorial articles (~2,000-2,200 words each), each AI-citation eligible, each internally linked to its commercial counterpart. Phase 2 of the 90-day plan.

Compliance + Risk

Florida cosmetic surgery has rules. We work inside them.

Operating in Florida cosmetic surgery means operating under the Florida Board of Medicine + FDA + FTC + ASPS Code of Ethics simultaneously. Three flags inform every piece of copy and every line of schema we produce.

Flag 01

No superlatives

"Best," "safest," "guaranteed," "leading" are off-limits in any copy AG Consulting produces, regardless of how true they may be. Third-party recognition (SRQ Top Doctors 2024, Sarasota Magazine 2025, Aesthetic Society membership) carries the credibility on the patient's behalf.

Flag 02

ABPS, always specified in full

Every credential reference says "ABPS board-certified" or "board-certified by the American Board of Plastic Surgery." Never just "board-certified." Florida lets cosmetic-only boards advertise under the same language, and the distinction matters legally and to patients.

Flag 03

No patient photos without consent

Every sample deliverable in this hub uses photography placeholders. Real patient before/after photos enter the live site only after signed photographic-release documentation is captured at kickoff.
